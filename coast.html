Coast to Coast

tl;dr

I spent 10 weeks designing, prototyping, and manufacturing a set of bronze sandcast coasters, with minerals embedded in epoxy and a finished wood inlay.

October - December 2019

Role: Designer and Manufacturer

Tools: SOLIDWORKS, sandcasting, woodworking, 3D-printing

Problem

I wanted to create meaningful, high-quality gifts for the most avid (and important) tea drinkers in my life: my mom and dad! My main design goal was to incorporate a sense of togetherness while I am thousands of miles away from them. I also wanted to pay homage to their love for gardening and plants, which led to the cherry blossom (common around our hometown) and palm tree (common around campus) details. Practically speaking, the wood element was an elegant solution to protect surfaces from being scratched.

Process

Sketches and Rapid Prototypes

During the initial stages of ideation, I brainstormed dozens of potential project ideas. For my top 3 ideas after consultation with my class mentor, I sketched a couple drawings of the product's meaning and projected scope.

My first rapid prototyping attempt led me to Room 36, the "digital prototyping" hub of the Stanford Product Realization Lab. I laser-cut a couple of coasters out of 1/8th-inch Duron. Each coaster had a removable center piece, which allowed me to visualize the negative space that I intended to fill with epoxy resin or another piece.

CAD Models and Operations Sequence

Creating detailed CAD models that included draft angles, fillet radii, and accurate dimensions proved to be immensely helpful since they were directly used to generated the 3D-printed pieces for my pattern. I iterated through 10 (yes, 10!) versions of each CAD model (California and Virginia), adjusting dimensions and angles to allow for cleaner pulls in the sandcasting process (see below). Design

Just outside my window, I saw the tops of palm trees sway in the breeze, and I was suddenly reminded of my parentsâ€™ love for plants and gardening. I followed this thread of inspiration, which led to the calming palm trees and cherry blossoms you will find in my final product.

Manufacturing Process

I handmade a custom pattern board, gate, and runner for my patterns in the woodshop. Shellac and the spindle sander became my best friends in this process, as I had to make sure the draft angles of the gate and runner were perfectly compatible with my patterns, and that the wood of the board wasn't too porous.

The reason I had to make 10 versions of each CAD model was to ensure that my patterns would pull cleanly from the sand when I rammed sand on them. The ramming process itself was surprisingly fun and also a great workout - essentially, you're packing pounds and pounds of sand on top of your pattern board in order to imprint the pattern into the sand. This requires an increasing amount of force as the layers get taller, while maintaining delicacy so that the patterns don't move around. A helpful metaphor is to imagine each layer of sand like tectonic plates which, when rubbed against each other, can cause the entire product to crack.

A clean pull maintains all the fine detail of the original pattern, and doesn't have any "wash," or areas where the sand fell apart and crumpled. Because my pulls from my first few iterations of patterns indicated that some aspects of the designs were too thin (and thus very fragile when the pull was lifted), I had to innovate on the design in thoughtful ways.

Once I was happy with how the sand pulls and bronze pours turned out, I had to spend an overwhelming amount of time sanding the surface of my coasters. Starting with 60-grit sandpaper, then moving to 80, then 150, 200, 400, 600, 800, 1000, and finally 1,200, I spent hours and hours sanding away at the surface of my coasters to achieve a spotless shine. This was the most incredible and rewarding, though tiresome and frustrating, part of the entire process. It was critical that I be patient at the lower grits in order to smooth out any kinks in the bronze. If I ever moved up to a higher grit too soon, I'd have to start all over at the lower grits to get the imperfections out (since the higher grit sandpaper could no longer smooth out larger divets). At the end of it all, I polished the bronze with some wax paste and admired the mirror-like reflectivity on the surface!

I split my time between sanding and woodworking, since I also decided to inlay my pieces in a wooden frame. The purpose of this wood frame was twofold, as I wanted to add an element of wood-metal interaction while at the same time providing functional value to the coasters with a base that would protect surfaces from being scratched. The process of creating this wooden frame included sawing the wood, routing the middle radius out, sanding it to smooth edges, and finally staining and sealing to make it liquid-proof. After all, these are coasters that must withstand spills and washes!
