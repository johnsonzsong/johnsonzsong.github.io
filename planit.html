Planit

tl;dr

My team and I created an Android app to make travel planning fun while instilling confidence in trustworthy plans. Planit gamifies the travel-planning process by emphasizing the strong ties among family and friends.

September - December 2018

Role: Android Developer, Web Developer, and Designer

Tools: Android Studio (C++), Figma

Problem

We sought to help travelers overcome the familiar struggles of planning for their destination trips: not being confident in their plans without the input and approval of family and friends, who were generally unmotivated to respond.

Process

Needfinding

When we first started our project, we had no idea what problem we would be addressing. Our first step was to find our what users' experiences were in the travel space. Through interviews with a diverse variety of travelers, airline employees, planners, parents, and more, we uncovered the need for efficient and engaging ways to plan trips. Below is a picture of me and a teammate unpacking our findings through an empathy map. You can also click to see our full needfinding results.

Needfinding Results

Experience Prototypes

Next, we used our initial insights from this first set of interviews to further test assumptions. We conducted another round of interviews, and created Point-of-View personas detailing our surprises and game changers. From this, we generated dozens of "How Might We" statements and used them as jumping points to ideate solutions.

We created rapid prototypes for 3 of our top solutions, which were:

  • Likes and Reviews for Plans
  • Augmented Reality Preview of Itinerary
  • Family Feud Style Competition for Generating Ideas

We tested these out with willing participants and recorded our findings. Check out some of the behind-the-scene photos below or read the full report of our experience prototypes!

Experience Prototype Results

Brainstorming and Lo-Fi Prototype

We received the most favorable feedback in our "competition/game" solution, and proceeded to make a Concept Video to prove its potential. See the video for yourself! This was a really fun exercise that made us consider the solution in a different light by exaggerating and dramatizing it. Below is a behind-the-scenes photo of our storyboarding process for the video.

In thinking about the style and gameplay of our eventual game, we engaged in a brainstorming session called "Crazy 8s" to generate many different ideas in a short amount of time.

After this, we created a low fidelity paper prototype for the user flow and interactions that would occur in the different screens of our mobile app. The purpose of this lo-fi prototype was to begin to think more critically about the exact interactions (swipes, gestures, clicks, etc.) that users would experience throughout the app. The use of paper and pen allowed us to quickly modify and adjust screens based on feedback.

Med-Fi Prototype and Evaluation

After testing our lo-fi paper prototype with recruited participants, we refined our initial sketches and began to develop wireframes using Figma. You might have caught a glimpse of it at the top of this page! With this functional prototype, we were able to observe others interacting with it via a screen, which simulated the real scenario in which the app would be used. This allowed us to develop a much better sense of what was working well and what wasn't, through a process called heuristic evaluation. Below, you can see some wireframes that were part of our med-fi prototype.

Solution

The final step was taking all of our needfinding and design work and turning it into a fully-functional Android app. For this, our team used Android Studio and programmed in C++ to make the game come to life. We showcased this at the project fair, and you can see the poster we made with an overview of our entire process and final product!
