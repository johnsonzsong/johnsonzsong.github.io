Bite-Size

tl;dr

My team and I conducted multiple rounds of needfinding, prototyping, and testing in order to design an interactive hi-fidelity prototype of a mobile app to address the issues that students, the Haas Center for Public Service, and external community organizations had volunteer participation.

April - June 2019

Role: Product Designer

Tools: Figma, Journey Maps, Personas, Prototypes

Problem

While students at Stanford often indicate interest in volunteering opportunities at the Haas Center for Public Service, Stanford's hub for fostering service, scholarship, and community, they have trouble following through because of other commitments. Our team wanted to answer the question: how might we enable students to find opportunities that integrate their academic interests, career goals, and desire to serve surrounding communities?

Process

Needfinding and Journey Maps

We began by interviewing the key players in the Haas Center ecosystem. After 4 interviews with students, 4 interviews with community organizations that partner with the Haas Center, and 4 interviews with Haas Center staff, we came up with actor theories for each key player. From these, we created journey maps and personas that represented our initial findings. Below is the journey map that I created for the Haas Center staff.

Ideation

Our team created dozens of "how might we" statements that represented the pain points of our key players. We aggregated the most moving HMW statements in a single one: "How might we enable students to find opportunities that integrate their academic interests, career goals, and desire to serve surrounding communities?" With this in mind, we moved to the solution space to start brainstorming.

Rapid Experimentation

We chose three ideas from our initial ideation stage to dive deeper into, one of which included the notion of "bite size volunteering." With each idea, we performed rapid experimentation for some quick and dirty results that would inform the rest of our project. For example, below is the experiment outline for the "bite size volunteering" experiment.

Comparator Study

Our larger team then split into two smaller groups to pursue one of those three ideas further. My partner (Kristina) and I decided to run with this fascinating idea of "bite size service." As part of the design process, we turned to existing apps and experiences that shared characteristics with the vision we had for our solution app. These comparator studies allowed us to focus on specific aspects of the experience we wanted students to have when viewing volunteer opportunities. That's why we chose to observe how our user demographic interacted with Facebook Events, Handshake, and Carta.

Ideation (Round 2)

Our results from the comparator studies allowed us to start brainstorming solutions, this time thinking about rough interfaces and design patterns we might want to evoke in our app. Below is one example of a paper prototype that Kristina and I created and tested with some students!

Wireframes

The last couple of weeks of the course were spent continuously refining our wireframes (on Figma) through continued interviews and experiments. We practiced the RITE testing method, which stands for Rapid Iterative Testing and Evaluation, which allowed us to quickly adjust the wireframes based on evolving findings. Our initial wireframes looked like this.

Branding

Alongside RITE testing, we had a lot of fun on our branding exploration. We used mood boards, color theory, and more to decide on a unifying theme for the app. Our final visual design system is pictured below, whose elements are chosen to reflect a modern take of the classic "Stanford" colors. We arrived at this aesthetic because we wanted to maintain the authority and trustworthiness given off by the official Stanford cardinal palette, while freshning up the feel of our app.

Solution

Our final solution was a hi-fidelity prototype of an app that presents students with highly-customized, "Bite-Size" volunteering opportunities in a smooth user interface. Click through our prototype below!
